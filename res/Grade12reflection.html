Maze Project: Designed a 4-sided 3D Maze for the freshman exploratory.

My teacher, Mr. Trottier, who has been a large influence and knowledgeable educator, assigned a task for the seniors with the intent of creating a new project for the freshman to work on during exploratory. As my responsibility, I was tasked with creating a 4-sided 3D “maze” with specific parameters and constraints. As the complexity of the project and its corresponding constraints became more prevalent, the project gradually became more challenging. At first, the requirements seemed manageable, but as additional details, limitations, and design considerations were introduced, the scope of the work expanded significantly. Each new constraint required careful thought and adjustment to the original plan, making the process more demanding than initially expected. However, instead of viewing these difficulties as setbacks, I chose to approach them with a positive mindset and treat them as valuable learning experiences. I took this challenge as an opportunity, since overcoming these obstacles is contributing to my personal growth as a future engineer. Engineering often involves solving problems that do not have obvious or immediate solutions, and this project allowed me to experience that process firsthand. By working through the complexities and adapting to new requirements, I was able to strengthen my problem-solving skills and develop a deeper understanding of how engineering projects evolve over time. These experiences helped me become more confident in my ability to approach difficult tasks and think creatively when facing unfamiliar challenges. Being able to experiment with different ways to achieve an end product is not always straightforward. Many of the decisions required evaluating multiple possible approaches and determining which one would be the most effective within the project’s limitations. Since the task was largely open-ended, there was no single correct path to follow. Instead, the process required making a series of thoughtful decisions, testing ideas, and sometimes revising earlier choices in order to improve the final outcome. This type of decision-making requires strong critical thinking skills and a willingness to learn from both successes and mistakes. In addition to the design and problem-solving aspects of the project, I was also assigned the responsibility of writing a technical procedural document for freshmen to use when creating their own maze. This task required me to clearly organize the steps involved in the process so that someone with less experience could easily follow them. Writing the document encouraged me to think carefully about each stage of the project and how to communicate technical information in a clear and accessible way. Overall, this responsibility allowed me to contribute to the learning experience of future students while also improving my own technical communication skills.

Dirty Hands Project Wood Brand

In October of my senior year, collaborative teamwork and skills were established to complete a project for Dr. Ricco, where the Dirty Hands Project foundation’s logo was burned and set onto a piece of wood for use as a coaster. This project uses the core skills of manufacturing to collaboratively and productively use these skills to complete a real world project. During these projects, me and my other shop members worked together to create two fixtures that would press the logo onto the wood at a precise and even temperature.

The first step that took place for the project was to complete the branding iron, made from steel, that would place the logo reversed and outset from the steel surface. The format of the design was initially an JPEG file, however, since a JPEG file uses pixels to store the information, it wouldn’t be useful for creating toolpaths. However, there are algorithms designed to scan the pixels of a raster image and convert them into a vector format. We used an algorithm to convert the raster image to a Scalable Vector Graphics (SVG) file that can be used with Fusion360. The file was imported into Fusion360 on a simple 2D block that represented the steel we have in stock, and size and position was adjusted accordingly. The extrude feature was used to extrude the sketch (from the svg) to the appropriate height. The model was completed, however the model needed to be CAMed for use as a toolpath. The CAM was simple, however, the huge problem with this project was how intricate the details were in the logo. Due to their details, not all of them could be represented with a reasonable size end mill. However, due to these limitations, we were able to successfully mill the surface very slowly using an extremely tiny end mill. Using the feature titled rest machine, we were able to resist the toolpath to only include the surfaces that have not been milled yet, reducing the cycle time, and cutting any inefficiencies in the program. This requires entering a tool diameter that was used with the previous tool to mark the leftover material where it needs to be cut. At first, a half inch end mill is used to quickly remove material from the outside. Next, an eighth inch end mill was used to remove some of the details in the logo, then a fifty-five thousandths end mill was used to remove most of the leftover material, and finally a thirty two thousandths end mill to finish the part. This process involved making sure the clearance height was set to something very low, so that it wouldn’t waste cycle time slowly plunging. However, it needed to be above the amount of offset that is set using Z “Work Offset” for a convenient way to take more material. In total, thirty thousandths of material was used for the height. Also, aluminum handles were manually milled and were attached to the fixture using a drill and a tap.

Furthermore, more needed to be done to create a practical and efficient set up for branding the wood. The students, together, created a wood insulator to attach to the handles to insulate the heat and also to provide a comfortable and practical way to hold the fixture when burning. The insulator consists of a small wooden block with four through holes and a center hole that is tapped, and a wooden handle that is threaded on a lathe to screw into the center hole. This handle enhances the process, further insulating the material from hands, and also being comfortable. Additionally, another fixture was made to hold the wood block at a fixed location that is perfectly square. A big material (at least an inch thick) was milled to create a 4.05”x4.05” pocket using a ⅜ end mill. This pocket has a corner radius due to the radius of the tool, however, the radius can be abolished by milling straight downwards in all four corners of the pocket using the center of the tool. In addition, a wall was added to ease with alignment and placement by milling two holes at the edge of each of the pocket edges facing in the top-most leftwards direction, Dowl pins were press-fitted into each milled hole to create points representing the corners in which the part is placed to assist with alignment. Finally, the wood blocks were finally ready to be branded. A laser was used to detect the heat currently applied to the branding iron. A torch was used to heat up the surface to 900 degrees. Initially, the temperature was around 600 but we raised the temperature to 900 for more perfect and even heating. The first block was burned for approximately 3 seconds, then the next 10, then the next 15 or 20. This was done so the foreseeing parts can be branded without having to re-heat the surface of the branding iron.

Haas / Fanuc Robot

During my senior year, a project assigned specifically to me was to program the Haas (Fanuc) robot to load a part into the machine. As both me and my teacher, Mr. Trottier was unsure of the process, so we worked together to figure out how to operate the robot and eventually program the robot using a technique that was safe and effective.

First, jogging was achieved on the robot using Current Commands -> Devices -> Robot -> Jogging, this allowed the robot to be moved manually without automation. Next, under Current Commands -> Devices -> Robot -> Setup, the procedure for programming the robot could take place. The procedure for programming the robot involved following the instructions on where to move the robot and programming the position of the robot as parameters for the machine to generate the program. However, additional procedures were in place to ensure that the operations completed by the robot were correct. For example, the initial position needs to be recorded for the program to run backwards when unloading the part, as this will prevent the door from closing into the arm. Furthermore, more procedures such as taking the part in and out of the vise manually using the foot pedal, using the gripper, recovering the robot back to home position, adjusting frames, and getting a feel for the user interface and menus for interacting with the robot was essential to comprehending the robot and the Haas interface.

As similar to the maze, an instructional document was written to document the procedure. To do this, I took photographs of the robot, the interface menu, and the location values and used them to create a step-by-step guide on how to operate the Haas Robot. This time, the document was aimed to be more technical, since it was not designed for freshmen to read. The problem we had was the process was not documented, and therefore we were forgetting everything. This instructional document should provide an easy way to understand the information and procedures at any time without the worry of losing information.

The learning Haas Robot is important because it has the potential to lead to high-skill manufacturing jobs due to its importance in automation. Having workers who are knowledgeable on the complex design of the systems in the factory can better understand how to react to problems that occur in the workplace.

My role at the Fall 2025 Open House was to introduce the Haas Robot, briefly demonstrate its programming and interface, and show the loading and finished part pick-up motions. There was a high level of interest in the Haas Robot within the Advanced Manufacturing program. This was the first time the Haas Robot had been shown to potential students at an Essex Tech Open House. Prior to the event, I worked with my teacher to learn how to navigate the menus and operate the robot.

Advanced Manufacturing Arboriculture Tree Signs

The goal of this project was to make 30 signs representing the trees of Essex North Shore’s Arboriculture Maplewood Arboretum. Each sign contains the name of the tree in English, and an additional subtext that contains the name in Latin. Underneath, there is a machined QR code that links to a google document containing more information for the tree. Additionally, it contains a logo and the text “Maplewood Arboretum”.

Due to the previous shortcomings of the previous signs, they quickly faded and wore off. As a result, these new signs are fully machined, and therefore are not printed and do not suffer from the same failures.  One of the project’s major successes was the QR codes. The QR codes were machined with a milling machine, so they will not wear out over time. They also contain error correction, so they are resistant to dirt or debris. They work perfectly and scan from a wide range of distances.

QR Codes are very complex from a topological and graphical complexity perspective. As the white and black spots form contrast, machining a tool path with an End Mill or other tool will be difficult due the small size needed to achieve such resolution with traditional machining operations such as a pocket or profile. As a result, software such as Fusion360 will be very suboptimal at automating the toolpath generation of 30 different QR codes. Fusion360 works with different size tools, and can’t break free from the more common workflow of machine operations. As a result, I took my previous skills I had as a hobby software developer (C++), and wrote a program that takes all 30 QR codes, and uses a QR code generation library that generates the code. I used this program to then generate the G-code (toolpath) for each of the QR codes in an automated way. Creating the QR codes from a website, converting all 30 of them into vector-based files, and importing all of them individually into Fusion360 by

importing the image into the sketch is very time consuming and disgusting to think about. My program optimizes this by parsing a text file containing all of the tree names and links, and generates the QR code at the same time it generates the G-code. Since engraving tools and spot drills can have varying visible diameter due to their nature of having a depth that can be changed, they make for a great option as a tool for engraving QR codes. Small end mills can be expensive, but the engraving tool does not need to be small due to its ability to only have a tiny radial portion of the tool engraved

into the material. My program has a “for” loop, which loops through each of the spots, or modules, on a QR code on the X and Y axis, and does simple arithmetic to calculate

the corresponding X and Y positional coordinates for the machine. It accumulates a string based on these positional values with corresponding G-code syntax prefixed to their operations. The depth can be calculated by dividing the optimal diameter by 2, because the X is proportional to the Y with a 45 degree tool (as tan(45 deg) = 1). The division by 2 occurs because the diameter is double the radius. This program was also optimized to save on cycle time by keeping the rapid distance to a minimum.

I’ve found that my accumulation of knowledge regarding software and mathematics to be very useful and essential to this project. C++ and software is not anywhere near the

agenda, curriculum, or expectations for the shop program. However, it was necessary to avoid taking less optimal shortcuts or wasting time on this project. I’ve also found

that the collaboration between me and the other people working on this project in the shop will be very beneficial. As I worked on all of the front informational plates, the others worked on getting all of the aluminum to size and ready to be assembled. Additionally, the collaboration from the machines in Metal Fabrication and some of Carpentry was necessary for some of the tasks that this project required. Most obviously, the surplus of help and education I have received over the course of my shop years has trained me to this level, and my patience and dedication to participation in the shop exhibits my abilities.

These elements were mounted on an aluminum base through bolts and “thru holes” to mount it very firmly. The aluminum base contains a base with set screws for mounting to the ground at a specific angle. This was attached through tig welding.

Fusion360 was used for all of the other elements of the front-facing plate, such as the text elements and the logo. Fusion360 is very useful and time-saving, but not for the

QR codes. The material for the front-facing plate was a plastic that was dual-layer, which allows for contrast between two depths. These signs were a collaboration between us, Metal Fabrication, and partially Carpentry for Arboriculture.

This project was completed according to expectations, and Essex Tech Arboriculture was very thankful and content with the project. As this was a favor rather than a requirement, it meant a lot to them, and to the values for our shop. They will be successfully mounted in the ground for many years of Essex Tech’s future history.
